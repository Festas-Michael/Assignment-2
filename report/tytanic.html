Part 1 · Data Cleaning

Reading the Gaps

Before any model can learn from this data, we must confront what's missing — and make deliberate, principled decisions about how to handle it.

Part 2 · Feature Engineering

Building What the Data Implies

The raw columns are just the surface. Beneath them lie richer signals — family dynamics, social standing, physical proximity to lifeboats — all recoverable through careful engineering.

Derived Features

FamilySize
SibSp + Parch + 1
Captures the full travelling group. Solo travellers and very large families both show lower survival rates — revealing a sweet-spot of 2–4.
IsAlone
1 if FamilySize == 1
Binary flag for solo travellers. Travelling alone meant no family member to help secure a lifeboat spot.
Title
Name.split(',')[1].split('.')[0]
Extracted from Name field. Maps to: Mr, Mrs, Miss, Master, Rare_Male, Rare_Female. Encodes both gender and social rank simultaneously.
Deck
Cabin[0] or 'U' (unknown)
First letter of Cabin. Upper decks (A–C) were 1st class and closer to lifeboats. 'U' = missing cabin = mostly 3rd class.
AgeGroup
Child / Teen / Adult / Senior
Ordinal bucketing of Age. Children (<13) had priority boarding. Seniors (>60) had reduced mobility and lower survival.
FarePerPerson
Fare / FamilySize
Normalises Fare by group size — a £40 ticket for four is very different from a £40 solo fare. Better proxy for true wealth.
IsWoman_or_Child
(Sex == 'female') | (AgeGroup == 'Child')
Encodes the "women and children first" lifeboat rule as an explicit engineered signal — the strongest domain-knowledge feature.
LogFare & LogAge
log1p(Fare), log1p(Age)
Log transforms reduce right-skew and make distributions approximately normal — essential for distance-based and linear models.
Interaction Features
Pclass × Fare, Age × Pclass
Captures compound effects — a wealthy 1st-class passenger (high Pclass×Fare) had compounding survival advantages vs a poor 3rd-class child.

Encoding Strategy

FeatureEncodingWhy
SexOne-hotNominal; no ordinal relationship
EmbarkedOne-hotNominal port codes (S/C/Q)

Part 3 · Feature Selection

Cutting the Noise

More features isn't always better. Redundant and correlated features add noise, slow training, and hurt generalisation. We use a two-step filter to arrive at the optimal subset.

Step 1 — Correlation Filter (threshold: |r| > 0.90)

Highly correlated feature pairs are identified; from each pair, the feature with the lower absolute correlation to the target (Survived) is removed. This dropped 2 redundant features — Sex_female (redundant with Sex_male) and Deck_U (strongly correlated with Cabin_known).

Step 2 — Random Forest Importance (300 trees, max_depth=8)

A Random Forest was trained on the 35 post-correlation features. Features are ranked by Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI). The 12 features above the mean importance threshold are selected for the final model.

Key Findings

What the Data Reveals

The Gender Gap Was Stark

Female passengers survived at 74.8% vs 22.3% for males — a 3.4× difference. Sex is the single most important predictive feature.

Class Was Life or Death

1st class had 55.5% survival, 2nd class 44.6%, 3rd class 33.8%. But class worked differently by gender — 1st-class women had ~95% survival; 3rd-class men around 12%.

Title Encodes Hidden Signal

Master (young boys) survived far better than Mr (adult men). Title simultaneously captures gender AND social standing in a single engineered feature.

Family Size Is Non-Linear

Travelling with 1–3 family members improved survival odds. Solo travellers and families of 5+ saw significantly lower rates — making FamilySize a richer signal than SibSp or Parch alone.

Deck Proxies for Class

Upper decks (A–C) had higher survival. The 'U' (unknown) deck, comprising passengers without a recorded cabin, skews heavily toward 3rd class and shows the lowest survival.

Interaction Features Add Value

Age_x_Pclass ranked 4th in importance — capturing that old age was more penalising in lower classes, and youth more protective in upper classes.
